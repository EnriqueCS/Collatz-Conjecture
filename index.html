Collatz Conjecture

\( \forall n \in \mathbb{Z}^+, \exists K \in \mathbb{Z}^+ : f^K(n) = 1 \), where:

\[ f(n) = \begin{cases} \frac{n}{2}, & n \text{ even} \\ 3n + 1, & n \text{ odd} \end{cases} \]

Partitioning \( \mathbb{Z}^+ \)

Let \( i \in \{ 0, 1, 2 \} \).

\[ \mathbb{Z}^+ = A_0 \sqcup A_1 \sqcup A_2 \quad A_i = \{ x \in \mathbb{Z}^+ \mid x \equiv i \, (\text{mod} \, 3) \} \] \[ E_i = \{ x \in A_i \mid x \text{ is even} \}, \quad O_i = \{ x \in A_i \mid x \text{ is odd} \} \] \[ E = E_0 \sqcup E_1 \sqcup E_2, \quad O = O_0 \sqcup O_1 \sqcup O_2 \quad E \sqcup O = \mathbb{Z}^+ \]
Set \( E_n \) Properties of \( x \)
\( E_0 \) \( x = 2k_1 \ \land \ x = 3k_2 \)
\( E_1 \) \( x = 2k_1 \ \land \ x = 3k_2 + 1 \)
\( E_2 \) \( x = 2k_1 \ \land \ x = 3k_2 + 2 \)
Set \( O_n \) Properties of \( x \)
\( O_0 \) \( x = 2k_1 + 1 \ \land \ x = 3k_2 \)
\( O_1 \) \( x = 2k_1 + 1 \ \land \ x = 3k_2 + 1 \)
\( O_2 \) \( x = 2k_1 + 1 \ \land \ x = 3k_2 + 2 \)

\[ n \xrightarrow{\rightarrowtail} 3n + 1 \quad \text{if } n \text{ is odd} \] \[ n \xrightarrow{\twoheadrightarrow} \frac{n}{2} \quad \text{if } n \text{ is even} \]

Proving \( E_0 \twoheadrightarrow_k O_0 \)

Let \( n \in E_0 \), so \( n = 2k_1 \land n = 3k_2 \); iterating \( \twoheadrightarrow \) yields odd \( m = 3k' \in O_0 \), thus \( E_0 \twoheadrightarrow_k O_0 \), \( \square \).

Proving \( O \rightarrowtail_1 E_1 \)

Let \( n \in O \), so \( n = 2k_1 + 1 \); applying \( \rightarrowtail \) gives \( 3n + 1 = 6k_1 + 4 \in E_1 \), thus \( O \rightarrowtail_1 E_1 \), \( \square \).

Proving \( A_0 \Rrightarrow_k A_1 \) or specifically \( A_0 \Rrightarrow_k E_1 \)

From \( E_0 \twoheadrightarrow_k O_0 \) and \( O_0 \rightarrowtail_1 E_1 \), starting from \( n \in E_0 \), we reach \( m \in O_0 \) via \( \twoheadrightarrow_k \), then \( m \rightarrowtail_1 E_1 \), thus \( A_0 \Rrightarrow_k E_1 \), \( \square \).

Proving \( E_2 \twoheadrightarrow_k E_1 \)

Let \( n \in E_2 \), so \( n = 2k_1 \land n \equiv 2 \ (\text{mod} \ 3) \); iterating \( \twoheadrightarrow \) yields \( m \in E_1 \), thus \( E_2 \twoheadrightarrow_k E_1 \), \( \square \).

Proving \( A_2 \Rrightarrow_k A_1 \) or specifically \( A_2 \Rrightarrow_k E_1 \)

From \( E_2 \twoheadrightarrow_k E_1 \) and \( O_2 \rightarrowtail_1 E_1 \), starting from \( n \in A_2 \), we reach \( m \in E_1 \) via \( \twoheadrightarrow_k \) or \( \rightarrowtail_1 \), thus \( A_2 \Rrightarrow_k E_1 \), \( \square \).

Proving Everything Ends in \( E_1 \)

From the above proofs, all paths from \( A_0 \), \( A_1 \), and \( A_2 \) eventually lead to \( E_1 \). Specifically:

  • \( A_0 \Rrightarrow_k E_1 \),
  • \( A_1 \) (via \( O_1 \rightarrowtail_1 E_1 \)),
  • and \( A_2 \Rrightarrow_k E_1 \).
Thus, all elements in \( \mathbb{Z}^+ \) eventually map to \( E_1 \), \( \square \).

Power of 2 Number Expression
\( 2^4 \) 16 \( 5 \times 3 + 1 \)
\( 2^5 \) 32 \( 3 \times 10 + 2 \)
\( 2^6 \) 64 \( 3 \times 21 + 1 \)
\( 2^7 \) 128 \( 3 \times 42 + 2 \)
\( 2^8 \) 256 \( 3 \times 85 + 1 \)

Generalized rule for powers of 2 in terms of \( k_3 \):

\[ S_0 = \{ x \in \mathbb{Z}^+ \mid x = 2^{2k_3}, \, k_3 \in \mathbb{Z}^+ \} \subset E_1 \subset A_1, \] \[ S_1 = \{ x \in \mathbb{Z}^+ \mid x = 2^{2k_3 + 1}, \, k_3 \in \mathbb{Z}^+ \} \subset E_2 \subset A_2. \]

In the Collatz conjecture, since \( f^K(x) = 1 \) is eventually reached, we can work backwards using the \( \twoheadrightarrow \) operation. Powers of 2 lead to 1 due to \( \twoheadrightarrow \).

\( S = S_0 \cup S_1 \) forms the 'end set.' Two sequences emerge:

\[ E \twoheadrightarrow_{k_1} \Rrightarrow_{k_2} S \twoheadrightarrow_{k_m} 1 \quad \text{(starting with \( x \in E \))} \] \[ O \rightarrowtail_{k_1} \Rrightarrow_{k_2} S \twoheadrightarrow_{k_m} 1 \quad \text{(starting with \( x \in O \))} \]

The \( \twoheadrightarrow \) operation is stackable: dividing an even number by 2 can yield another even number. Specifically:

\[ \twoheadrightarrow_m \twoheadrightarrow_n = \twoheadrightarrow_{m+n} \]

The \( \twoheadrightarrow_k \) operation applies \( k \) times without specifying whether the result is even or odd. In contrast, \( \twoheadrightarrow^{(O)}_k \) applies \( k \) times, with the final result being odd. Formally:

\[ \twoheadrightarrow_k \quad \text{(\( k \) applications, result unspecified)} \] \[ \twoheadrightarrow^{(O)}_k \quad \text{(\( k \) applications, result odd)} \]

For the \( \rightarrowtail \) operation (defined for odd numbers), let \( x = 2k_1 + 1 \). Applying \( \rightarrowtail(x) = 3x + 1 \) yields: \[ \rightarrowtail(x) = 3(2k_1 + 1) + 1 = 6k_1 + 4 = 2(3k_1 + 2), \] which is always even. Since \( \rightarrowtail \) is only defined for odd numbers, it cannot be stacked.

\[ \begin{cases} E \twoheadrightarrow_{k_1} \Rrightarrow_{k_2} S \twoheadrightarrow_{k_m} 1 & \text{(starting with \( x \in E \))} \\ O \rightarrowtail_{k_1} E' \twoheadrightarrow_{k_2} \Rrightarrow_{k_3} S \twoheadrightarrow_{k_m} 1 & \text{(expanded, where \( E' \) is the even result of \( \rightarrowtail \))} \end{cases} \]

The first case is a subsequence of the second, so we focus on the second case. Both sequences share the same structure after \( E' \), allowing us to study the general form: \[ O \rightarrowtail_{k_1} E' \twoheadrightarrow_{k_2} \Rrightarrow_{k_3} S \twoheadrightarrow_{k_m} 1, \]

We refine \( \twoheadrightarrow_k \) by introducing \( \twoheadrightarrow_k^{(O)} \), explicitly indicating an odd final outcome. This adjustment enhances the clarity and systematic structure of transitions.

\[ O \rightarrowtail_{k_1} E' \twoheadrightarrow_{k_2}^{(O)} O' \Rrightarrow_{k_3} S^{(m-1)} \twoheadrightarrow_{k_m}^{(O)} 1 \]

Since \( O' \Rrightarrow_{k_3} S^{(m-1)} \) maps odd to even, \( \rightarrowtail \) alone suffices. However, an alternate sequence is:

\[ \rightarrowtail \twoheadrightarrow^{(O)} \rightarrowtail \twoheadrightarrow^{(O)} \rightarrowtail \dots \rightarrowtail (\twoheadrightarrow \rightarrowtail)_L \]

This captures alternating \( \rightarrowtail \) and \( \twoheadrightarrow^{(O)} \), transitioning odd to even until \( S^{(m-1)} \). (Note: \( L \geq 0 \).)

\[ O \rightarrowtail_{k_1} E' \twoheadrightarrow_{k_2}^{(O)} O' \rightarrowtail_{k_3} (\twoheadrightarrow \rightarrowtail)_L S^{(3 + 2L)} \twoheadrightarrow_{k_m}^{(O)} 1 \]

The sequence \( E' \twoheadrightarrow_{k_2}^{(O)} O' \rightarrowtail_{k_3} \) can be incorporated into \( L \). For example:

\[ 21 = 3 \times 7 = 2k + 1 \implies 21 \in O_0. \] Since \( 21 \times 3 + 1 = 64 = 2^6 \), only \( \rightarrowtail \twoheadrightarrow_{k_2}^{(O)} \) is needed to reach \( 1 \). Thus:

\[ O \rightarrowtail_{k_1} E' (\twoheadrightarrow \rightarrowtail)_L S^{(2L + 1)} \twoheadrightarrow_{k_m}^{(O)} 1 \] \[ (\rightarrowtail)_O (\twoheadrightarrow^{(O)} \rightarrowtail)_{L + O} \twoheadrightarrow^{(O)}_{L + O + N} 1 \]

This reduces the sequence while preserving transformation rules.

For \( (\twoheadrightarrow^{(O)} \rightarrowtail)_{L + O} \), each \( \twoheadrightarrow^{(O)} \) corresponds to steps \( n_1, n_2, \dots, n_l \) to become odd. Thus: \[ L = \sum_{i=1}^{l} (n_i + 1), \] where \( (n_i + 1) \) represents odd-to-even transitions followed by \( \rightarrowtail \).

\[ K = L + O + N, \] where:

  • \( L \): odd-to-even steps,
  • \( O \): 1 (odd start) or 0,
  • \( N \): steps to \( 2^N \).

Since all sequences eventually fall into \( E_1 \), we can reduce the structure by removing the odd part, simplifying it to: \[ (\twoheadrightarrow^{(O)} \rightarrowtail)_L \twoheadrightarrow^{(O)}_{L + N} 1 \]

We focus on the even sets \( E_1 \) and \( E_2 \). Since \( S \) (the end set) is already solved, we examine elements in \( E_1 \) and \( E_2 \) with one additional prime factor beyond 2.

For \( E_2 \), if an element is in \( S \), it is already solved. Otherwise, the sequence \( (\twoheadrightarrow^{(O)} \rightarrowtail)_L \) must occur, ending with \( \rightarrowtail \), which guarantees an even result in \( A_1 \subset E_1 \). Thus, the problem reduces to \( E_1 \).

The sequence can be represented as: \[ \left( \twoheadrightarrow^{(O)} \rightarrowtail \right)_L \twoheadrightarrow^{(O)}_{L + N} 1, \] where the bracketed sequence is guaranteed to occur at least once (unless the element is already in \( S \)).

Since \( \rightarrowtail \) in \( \left( \twoheadrightarrow^{(O)} \rightarrowtail \right)_L \) results in \( A_1 \), and for even powers of 2 (\( 2^{2k_3} \)), the form is \( 3k_2 + 1 \in A_1 \), we have: \[ \left( \twoheadrightarrow^{(O)} \rightarrowtail \right)_L = 2^{2n} = 4^n. \]

This operation is closed, sending \( E_1 \) into \( E_1 \) indefinitely.
